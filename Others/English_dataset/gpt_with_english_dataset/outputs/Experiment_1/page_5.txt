The new model, which has now been approved after revisions, gives users more control over how
their data is used [8]. Users can now choose between sharing all their data for highly
personalized ads or sharing less data for more general ads. This change will replace the old
“consent or pay” system, which required users to either accept data tracking for personalized ads
or pay to use the platforms without ads [8]. Without access to highly personal information,
Meta’s ads will rely on contextual targeting, which means backend engineering work to collect
different types of data in other ways. For advertisers, it could mean that they see weaker returns
with targeting becoming less efficient among users who have declined participation [8].

Henna Virkkunen, Executive Vice President for Technological Sovereignty, Security, and
Democracy, stated that the Digital Markets Act is built around protecting free choice for both
businesses and consumers [8]. She emphasized that individuals must have complete control over
how and when their personal data is used online, while companies should be able to communicate
freely with their customers [8].

The European Commission also stated that it will continue to monitor Meta’s compliance and
will collect feedback from Meta and other stakeholders once the model is implemented [8]. With
limited access to highly personal data, Meta’s ads will increasingly rely on contextual targeting,
meaning ads will be based more on content and context rather than detailed user profiling [8].

3.3 Legal Heat on Meta

European Union data protection law places strict obligations on platforms that process personal
data at scale, particularly when automated profiling and artificial intelligence are involved. The
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) requires that personal data be processed
lawfully, transparently, and for limited purposes (Article 5 GDPR) [9]. Platforms must
have a valid legal basis for processing data (Article 6) [10], ensure that consent is freely given
and informed (Articles 4(11) [11] and Article 7 [12]), and provide clear information about
how personal data is collected, combined, and analysed (Articles 12–14) [13- 15]. In the
context of AI profiling, the GDPR also limits automated decision-making that significantly
affects individuals (Article 22) [16] and grants users rights to access, rectify, and erase their
data. Together, these provisions are intended to give individuals meaningful control over their
online data trail, even when advanced AI systems are used to analyse their behaviour.
