However, Meta’s real-world data practices, as outlined in the previous sections, 
demonstrate persistent tension with these legal standards. The Cambridge 
Analytica case revealed that millions of Facebook users’ personal data was collected 
and used to create psychological profiles without valid consent, undermining the 
GDPR principles of lawfulness and transparency. Later enforcement actions by the 
Irish Data Protection Commission showed structural weaknesses in Meta’s data 
protection practices. The €265 million fine imposed in 2022 showed that Facebook’s 
design choices enabled large-scale data scraping, violating Article 25 GDPR [17] by 
failing to embed adequate privacy safeguards. Similarly, the record €1.2 billion fine 
in 2023 confirmed that Meta unlawfully transferred EU user data to the United 
States without sufficient protection, breaching Article 46 GDPR [18] and placing 
user data beyond effective EU control. At last, the €91 million fine for storing 
passwords in plain text illustrated failures to meet basic security obligations 
under the GDPR. Together, these cases show that although EU law provides strong 
rules to protect privacy, Meta’s use of AI-driven data collection and profiling makes 
it very difficult for users to truly control their online data trail. 

3.4 Tomorrow in a Fog: Privacy Lost in Algorithms 

Artificial intelligence mediates the relationship between users and digital 
platforms. From mobile phones and cars to online services, AI helps make daily 
tasks faster, easier, and more efficient. If artificial intelligence had to be described 
in three steps, they would be: (1) collecting large amounts of data, (2) using this data 
to train models, and (3) producing outcomes such as decisions or predictions. In 
other words, data collection is a key part of how AI systems learn and improve. 
Because of this process, users receive personalized content, useful recommendations,
and advertisements that match their interests. In many ways, data collection has 
made modern life more connected and convenient. 

However, AI profiling and constant data collection are far more risky than they 
first appear. When a digital service is offered for free, users often pay with their 
personal data instead of money. In this sense, users themselves become the product.
Meta’s core business model is built on collecting, analysing, and monetising 
people’s data. Despite receiving numerous fines and regulatory warnings, the 
company has continued to expand its data-driven practices, often pushing legal
and ethical boundaries.
