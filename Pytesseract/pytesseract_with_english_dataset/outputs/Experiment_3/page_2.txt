2. Meta’s Al-Driven Data Collection and Profiling

2.1 How Meta Officially Stores User Data

According to Mete’s official website, its privacy policy explains what information is collected from users and whyit is needed. This includes content
people share on Facebook, such as posts, messages, and profile details, as well as technical information from devices. Meta states that this data
is used to run the service properly, improve features, keep accounts safe, and show relevant content and advertisements [2]. The policy describes
different types of stored data in a clear way. This includes basic account details and shared content, information about how users interact with the
platform and details about connections like friends and followers. It also covers technical data, such as device information, IP addresses, cookies,
and location data when enabled. These data types help Meta understand how its services are used and ensure they work smoothly [2].

The update also aims to make privacy practices easier to understand. Meta explains that no new data collection methods are being introduced,
but existing ones are described more clearly. Users are informed about their rights, how their information may be shared, and how they can
control their privacy through available settings and tools [2].

2.2 Controversies Over Meta’s Secret Data Collection

Even though Meta explains its data collection procedures, as described in the previous section, in reality, its practices tell a different story. In March
2018, the Guardian reported that the data analytics firm Cambridge Analytica, which worked with Donald Trump's election team and the Brexit
campaign, harvested millions of Facebook profiles without user consent to build systems that could influence voter behaviour [3]. The data was
taken in early 2014 through a researcher's app and was used to build psychological profiles of individual users. This information was then used to
target political advertisements designed to influence people's choices at the ballot box [3].

Christopher Wylie, a former employee of Cambridge Analytica, revealed that Facebook data was used to create psychological profiles of users in
order to influence their political views [3]. He explained that this data was collected through a researcher's app and used to predict and target
people based on their emotions and behaviour. Internal Facebook documents later showed that by late 2015, the company was aware that large
amounts of user data had been collected improperly, but users were not clearly informed and the data was not fully protected [3].

After the misuse became public, Facebook suspended Cambridge Analytica and the researcher involved. However, at the time the issue was
discovered, Facebook initially minimized the seriousness of the situation and claimed the data collection followed normal procedures [3]. In reality,
sharing the data with third parties violated Facebook's own rules, which raised concerns about how well the company controlled access to user
information [3].

Concerns about Meta's data practices continued in later years. In November 2022, Meta was fined €265 million by the Irish Data Protection
Commission after an investigation found that Facebook features such as search functions and contact importer tools were designed without
sufficient privacy safeguards [4]. These features made it possible for large amounts of personally identifiable information (PII) such as names,
phone numbers, and email addresses of hundreds of millions of users to be collected and exposed through data scraping [4]. The DPC concluded
that Meta violated GDPR Article 25, which requires data protection by design and by default, because it failed to build adequate technical and
organizational measures into these systems to prevent misuse of user data [4].
