Even though Meta has paused this specific technique, it is likely that new tracking methods will
appear over time. Online privacy is harder to protect than it may seem. Privacy settings alone are
often not enough to stop companies like Meta from collecting user data, as they have shown that
built-in security protections on Android devices can be bypassed. One simple way to reduce
tracking is to use a more privacy friendly browser [6]. Chrome allows many tracking methods,
while browsers such as Firefox, Brave, DuckDuckGo, and Safari offer better protection. Android
users can set these browsers as their default, and Apple users can rely on Safari, even though no
browser can fully block all tracking. Using websites instead of mobile apps can also help protect
privacy. Apps usually have access to more personal information, such as location, device details,
and connected devices. For activities done only occasionally, like shopping or booking travel,
websites are generally the safer option [6].

Removing Meta apps from a phone can further limit data collection, as these apps allow deeper
access to user data [6]. Previous experiences show that these companies cannot be fully trusted,
as their apps allow them to collect more information than websites usually can. Even without
these apps, Meta can still collect data about what users do online [6]. This is because many
websites use Meta’s tracking tools. As a result, it is difficult for users to completely avoid being
tracked [6].

3.2 Meta Redesigns Its Data Strategy for European Users

With all the penalties it has faced, is Meta making a meaningful effort to reform its data practices?
After Meta reached a €200 million fine by EU in April 2025, the company decided to change its
rules for EU users. Meta was fined €200 million under the European Union’s Digital Markets Act
(DMA) [8]. The fine was issued because the EU stated that Meta’s “consent or pay” model does not
comply with the rules of the DMA [8]. Under this system, users of Facebook and Instagram had
to either agree to the merging of their personal data for personalized advertising or pay a
subscription fee to avoid ads [8]. The European Commission argued that this model does not
provide users with real and freely given consent, which violates DMA requirements.

In upcoming January 2026, following the satisfactory conclusion of a months-long Digital
Markets Act negotiation with the European Commission, Meta will offer European users on
Facebook and Instagram a new choice that allows reduced data sharing for less personalized
advertising [8]. This updated advertising system is only for the users in the European Union.
