In May 2023, another incident underscored again with Ireland’s Data Protection Commission (DPC), The DPC
fined Meta €1.2 billion for mishandling the personal data of European users, marking the largest penalty ever
under the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) [3]. The fine came after a long investigation into
how the company transferred user data from the EU to the United States of America without adequate
safeguards, despite a European Court of Justice ruling requiring strong protections for such transfers [5]. The
DPC found that Meta continued to send large volumes of Facebook users’ personal data to the USA on the basis
of standard contractual clauses (SCCs), that did not sufficiently protect individuals’ privacy rights, exposing
that data to potential access by US intelligence agencies. Because these clauses failed to meet GDPR standards,
the regulator concluded that Meta was in breach of Article 46(1) of the GDPR [10], which governs secure
international data transfers [5]. Also in October 2024, Meta was fined €91 million by the DPC again for storing
some Facebook users’ passwords in plain text within its internal systems. This practice did not meet basic
security standards, which require passwords to be protected using cryptographic methods [5].

2.3 Meta’s Silent Data Harvesting Algorithmic Practices Explained

During the early development of Meta’s advertising system, Meta introduced a small tracking script known as
the “Meta pixel” [7]. This technology is designed to observe and analyze user behavior, monitoring how visitors
navigate websites, interact with content, and respond to advertisements. In doing so, it can also transmit
highly sensitive information, such as financial data from tax filing platforms or medical details from healthcare
websites, directly into Meta’s surveillance-based advertising ecosystem [7].

In addition, Meta’s tracking pixel was secretly sending data to Meta’s own Android apps by exploiting localhost,
a feature intended for developer testing [7]. This bypassed Android’s app sandboxing, which is designed to
prevent apps from communicating with each other and created a hidden data channel between mobile browsers
and Meta apps.

At the same time, meta-attempts to convert data from “anonymous” website visitors to real Meta user
accounts by using signals such as IP addresses and cookies [7]. Meta made this identification much easier by
sending data directly from its tracking pixel to Meta’s own apps, where users were already logged in. This meant
that even users who blocked all external cookies, used a VPN to hide their IP address, or browsed in Incognito
mode could still be identified [7]. Once this practice was publicly revealed, Meta quickly paused the technique and
stated that they were in discussions with Google about “a potential miscommunication regarding the application
of their policies” [7].
